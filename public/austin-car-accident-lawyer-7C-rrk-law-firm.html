What is a contingency fee?

Personal injury lawyers often use a fee arrangement called a contingency fee. This means that if you hire a car accident lawyer on a contingency fee basis, you won't have to pay any money to the lawyer upfront or pay them an hourly fee. They will only receive a fee if:

You win your case.

A settlement for a car accident.

You have won a court case related to a car accident.

A contingency fee means that you only pay the lawyer if your case is successful. The fee is dependent on the outcome of the case.

Other Types of Car Accident Lawyer Fee Arrangements

The majority of lawyers work on a contingency fee basis for car accident lawsuits. This means clients do not have to pay upfront costs and won't have to pay if they lose the case. Instead, lawyers receive a portion of the compensation won as opposed to an hourly rate or a fixed fee.

Certain accident attorneys offer a flat-rate legal service, such as drafting a demand letter to be sent to the at-fault driver’s insurance company, for a fixed fee ranging from $300 to $1,000.

Contingency Fee Agreements Are for Petitioners Only

A contingency fee agreement is available only if an attorney is representing the person who filed the car accident lawsuit (petitioner).

Lawyers usually charge the defendant, who is being sued, an hourly rate that differs from lawyer to lawyer.

Typically, liability car insurance policies have a provision called "duty to defend." This means that if the policyholder is sued over an incident that the insurance covers, the insurance company is responsible for hiring and paying for a defense attorney.

Drivers without insurance or those whose coverage limits are exceeded must hire their attorney and pay an hourly fee, which can be quite costly (usually ranging from $150 to $500 per hour based on the location and the attorney's experience and reputation).

How Are Costs Handled in Car Accident Cases

Contingency fee agreements often advertise that clients won't have to pay if they don't win, but this may not always be the case. It's important to carefully review the agreement to determine if you could be responsible for paying your lawyer's expenses (also known as "costs") if you do not win the case. These costs can include expenses incurred during the investigation and pursuit of your car accident claim. Costs can include but are not limited to the following:

filing fees

photocopying fees

the cost of getting medical records and Car accident police reports

court reporter fees for depositions, and

expert witness fees.

It's important to note that expenses can swiftly accumulate and exceed thousands or tens of thousands of dollars. To avoid confusion, it is recommended that you clarify in a written contingency fee agreement how expenses will be managed.

Some personal injury attorneys may cover costs upfront and solely anticipate reimbursement if you win a settlement or a court verdict. However, most attorneys will require you to agree to pay some or all expenses if you do not win your case.

It is important to know when the costs will be deducted from your compensation. Check your agreement to see if the costs will be deducted before or after the lawyer's fee is calculated. If costs are deducted first, the lawyer's fee will be lower compared to when the fee is deducted first, and then costs are subtracted.

Suppose you have a legal case with $3,000 in costs and a $20,000 settlement, and you agreed to pay your lawyer a 33% contingency fee. If costs are subtracted from the settlement before calculating fees, your lawyer receives $5,610. However, if costs are subtracted after calculating fees, your lawyer receives $6,600.

To better handle expenses in a car accident case, it's important to have clarity between you and your lawyer. While certain costs like court filing fees are mandatory, expenses such as the investigator and expert witness fees can be decided by you and your lawyer.

It's important for your agreement to include a specified amount, above which your lawyer will need your permission for any additional expenses. This will prevent you from being surprised with an unanticipated bill at the end of your case.

How to Recover When an At-Fault Motorist Doesn't Have Insurance

If the driver at fault doesn't have insurance or their insurance isn't enough to cover your losses, there are two types of car insurance coverage that may help you after a car accident.

If you have uninsured motorist (UIM) coverage through your own car insurance policy (either because it's required in your state or because you added it to your policy), you can use it to pay for your medical bills and certain other losses related to a car accident caused by an uninsured driver.

Underinsured motorist coverage, also known as UIM, covers you when the driver at fault doesn't have enough liability insurance to pay for your accident-related losses. This part of your own insurance policy helps bridge the gap financially between your losses and the at-fault driver's coverage.

If you were in a car accident caused by someone else's negligence, you may be eligible to receive a significant amount of money to cover your medical expenses. In Texas, the minimum insurance coverage for drivers is $30,000 for injured persons and $25,000 for property damage in motorcycle accidents.

What If I am partly at fault for my car accident?

Texas follows modified comparative fault. This is the most common rule. It allows partially liable parties to seek compensation for their losses, but only if they are less at fault than the other party. Some states prohibit recovery if someone is 50% liable, while others set the limit at 51%

Why is it important to have a thorough conversation about your accident with a lawyer? Well, they can help you understand if you are eligible for financial compensation. Furthermore, a law firm has the ability to look into and analyze all the evidence related to the case in order to establish liability.

Other jurisdictions vary by state, but some common types of comparative fault recovery are as follows:

Pure Contributory Negligence – This is the harshest of the rules. States that follow the pure contributory negligence rule prohibit someone from recovering any compensation if they were even 1% at fault for the crash. This means that if both parties contributed to a car accident in any way, neither party can recover compensation from the other.

Pure Comparative Fault – Under these laws, you can seek recovery for partial losses even if you were 99% at fault! Parties who were partially at fault always have the opportunity to file a claim, as long as they were not 100% responsible.
