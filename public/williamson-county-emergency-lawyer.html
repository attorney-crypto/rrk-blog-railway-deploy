Common Williamson County emergencies

Criminal emergencies include arrests for DWI, assault (including family violence), drug possession, and theft offenses, often after traffic stops on I-35, US-183, or the SH-130 corridor. After booking at the Williamson County jail, the magistration process sets bond and conditions. Conditions set at this stage are difficult to change later.

Family-law emergencies include emergency protective orders following family violence arrests under Code of Criminal Procedure Article 17.292, temporary restraining orders in divorce and SAPCR cases under Texas Family Code Chapters 6 and 105, and motions for emergency custody where immediate child safety is at issue. These are heard in the Williamson County District Courts in Georgetown.

Civil emergencies in Williamson County include post-judgment proceedings, turnover orders, garnishments, and contempt show-cause hearings. Personal-injury emergencies include serious or fatal crashes on Williamson County roads and any case where physical or electronic evidence is at risk of being lost.

First steps after a Williamson County arrest

Use the right to remain silent and the right to counsel. Politely tell the officer that you want to speak with a lawyer before answering questions and stop discussing the incident. This rule applies during the stop, during transport to the Williamson County jail, and during booking. Statements made at any stage can be used later.

Do not consent to searches of your car, home, or phone. Saying "I do not consent to a search" preserves later challenges, even if officers proceed under another theory. Whether a search was lawful is a question a court can later decide on a motion to suppress.

After magistration, focus shifts to bond, conditions, and the first court setting in Georgetown. The Williamson County District Courts hear felonies; the County Courts at Law hear misdemeanors. Getting counsel involved before the next setting matters because the State has already begun assembling the case file from police reports, body camera footage, and any statements made.

Family-law emergencies in Williamson County

Emergency protective orders following a family violence arrest in Williamson County are typically issued under Code of Criminal Procedure Article 17.292 and can restrict contact, prohibit firearm possession, and require the respondent to stay away from a residence, school, or workplace. Violations create additional criminal exposure.

Temporary restraining orders in Williamson County divorce or SAPCR cases follow the Texas Family Code and the Texas Rules of Civil Procedure. Common forms include orders preserving the status quo on bank accounts and property, restraining harassment, and dealing with immediate custody and possession of children.

Emergency custody requests where a child is alleged to be in immediate danger require a specific factual showing and follow the procedure under the Texas Family Code. Mistakes in the procedure can cause orders to be set aside on review. Counsel before a hearing is set is more useful than counsel afterward.

Civil and post-judgment emergencies in Williamson County

A Texas judgment can be enforced in Williamson County through writs of execution, abstracts of judgment that create liens on real property, garnishment where permitted, and turnover orders under Civil Practice and Remedies Code Chapter 31. Each of these has its own response window and procedural rules.

Civil contempt proceedings for failure to comply with a court order can move on a short timeline. A show-cause hearing in a Williamson County court can be set within days of an alleged violation. Texas law distinguishes civil contempt (coercive) from criminal contempt (punitive), and the procedural protections differ.

Statutory deadlines for many post-judgment proceedings are short — 10, 14, or 30 days from service. Asserting exemptions, jurisdictional objections, or due-process challenges within the window is the difference between a real defense and a default loss.

Are these calls confidential even if I do not hire the firm?

Yes.
