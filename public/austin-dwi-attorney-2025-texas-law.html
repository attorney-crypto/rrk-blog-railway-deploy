2025–2026 TEXAS LAW

A first-offense DWI in Texas under Tex. Penal Code § 49.04 is a Class B misdemeanor carrying 72 hours to 180 days in jail and up to a $2,000 fine. A BAC of 0.15 or above elevates the charge to a Class A misdemeanor. A third DWI offense is a third-degree felony under Tex. Penal Code § 49.09. Texas law imposes automatic license suspension under the Administrative License Revocation program governed by Tex. Transp. Code § 724.035. A driver has 15 days from the date of arrest to request an ALR hearing or the suspension becomes automatic. 2025 Legislative Updates — 89th Texas Legislature (Effective September 1, 2025):

SB 745 amends Tex. Penal Code § 49.09(b-2): intoxication manslaughter is now a first-degree felony (5–99 years or life) when the defendant causes the death of more than one person in the same criminal transaction, or the death of a peace officer or EMS personnel. Single-victim, non-officer cases remain second-degree felonies (2–20 years).

SB 826 adds Tex. Penal Code § 49.04(e): DWI while operating a motor vehicle in a school crossing zone during the time the reduced speed limit applies is now a state jail felony (180 days–2 years; $10,000 fine max). This is a standalone enhancement within § 49.04 and does not merge with the child passenger enhancement under § 49.045. A defendant with a child passenger in a school crossing zone faces both provisions.

SB 1886 amends Tex. Code Crim. Proc. Art. 18.067: any Texas peace officer may now execute a blood-draw warrant in a county adjacent to where the warrant was issued, eliminating the prior requirement that the executing officer be individually authorized to make arrests in the county of execution.

SB 1660 amends Tex. Code Crim. Proc. Art. 38.50: crime labs must annually notify the prosecutor's office of all toxicological evidence held for offenses in that county. The prosecution has 90 days to provide written denial of a destruction request. Evidence must be preserved for the length of the defendant's sentence or supervision, or until acquittal or dismissal with prejudice if no conviction results.

SB 2320 — which would have upgraded first-offense DWI from a Class B to a Class A misdemeanor and DWI with BAC 0.15+ to a state jail felony — died in the House Criminal Jurisprudence Committee on May 22, 2025. It did not become law. First-offense DWI remains a Class B misdemeanor unless aggravating factors apply.

What happens after a DWI arrest in Austin?

You are booked, your license is confiscated and replaced with a temporary driving permit, and you have 15 days to request an ALR hearing with the Texas Department of Public Safety. Simultaneously, the Travis County District Attorney's office receives the case for prosecution. Both proceedings run parallel — the ALR is civil, the criminal case is separate.

What is the penalty for a first DWI in Texas?

Class B misdemeanor: 72 hours to 180 days jail, up to $2,000 fine, license suspension 90–365 days, and possible ignition interlock requirement. If BAC is 0.15 or above it becomes a Class A misdemeanor with up to one year jail and a $4,000 fine. DWI in a school crossing zone during reduced speed hours is now a state jail felony under SB 826 (effective September 1, 2025).

What is an ALR hearing and why does it matter?

The Administrative License Revocation hearing determines whether your license is suspended independent of the criminal case. More importantly, it gives your Austin DWI attorney the opportunity to cross-examine the arresting officer under oath before the criminal trial — locking in testimony that can be used for impeachment later.

Can a DWI be dismissed in Travis County?

Yes. Common grounds include unlawful traffic stop, improper field sobriety test administration, breathalyzer calibration errors, and blood draw chain of custody violations under Title 37 of the Texas Administrative Code. Suppression of the stop or the chemical test result frequently results in dismissal or significant charge reduction.

What changed about blood draw warrants in Texas in 2025?

SB 1886, effective September 1, 2025, allows any Texas peace officer to execute a blood draw warrant in an adjacent county without being individually authorized to make arrests in that county. This expands law enforcement's ability to collect blood evidence quickly. Defense counsel should review the executing officer's authority and the warrant's county of issuance on any DWI blood draw case arising after September 1, 2025.

What are the new tox evidence rules for DWI cases in Texas?

SB 1660, effective September 1, 2025, requires crime labs to notify the prosecutor annually of all retained toxicological evidence. The prosecution has 90 days to object in writing before evidence can be destroyed. If evidence is destroyed without proper notice or before the 90-day window closes, defense counsel should analyze the destruction under Brady, Trombetta, and Youngblood frameworks. No Texas appellate decisions have addressed SB 1660 yet as of April 2026.

How much does a DWI actually cost in Texas?

A first-offense DWI conviction realistically costs $10,000–$20,000 all in — fines, court costs, ignition interlock, DWI education program, SR-22 insurance surcharge, and attorney fees. The attorney fee is not the expensive part. The conviction is.
