2025–2026 TEXAS LAW

Texas is a community property state under Tex. Fam. Code § 3.002 — all property acquired during marriage is presumed equally owned by both spouses. Separate property owned before marriage or received by gift or inheritance during marriage belongs to the individual spouse but must be proven by clear and convincing evidence. Texas imposes a mandatory 60-day waiting period from the date of filing before a divorce can be finalized under Tex. Fam. Code § 6.702. Grounds for divorce in Texas include insupportability under Tex. Fam. Code § 6.001, cruelty, adultery, abandonment, felony conviction, living apart, and confinement in a mental hospital. Travis County divorce cases are filed in the Travis County District Clerk's office and heard in the 200th, 201st, 345th, or 126th District Courts. 2025 Legislative Updates — 89th Texas Legislature (Effective September 1, 2025):

C.S.H.B. 2643 increased the child support cap on net monthly resources from $9,200 to $11,700 — the largest increase in Texas history. The percentage guidelines (20/25/30/35/40%) are unchanged. The new cap applies to all cases pending in trial court on September 1, 2025 and all cases filed on or after that date.

SB 1559 amends Tex. Fam. Code §§ 81.012, 85.026(b), 85.064: a valid family violence protective order now prevails over any conflicting order in a pending divorce or SAPCR. Transfer of the protective order case to the divorce/SAPCR court is now mandatory upon motion (previously discretionary), unless the court finds transfer would negatively impact the safety of a protected person. Consolidation is not automatic — a motion by a party or the court is required to trigger transfer.

SB 1120 extends family violence protective orders to run until two years after the final divorce decree or final custody order, giving survivors more stability without returning immediately to court.

HB 2350 amends Tex. Fam. Code § 102.003: the standing threshold for non-parent caregivers to file a SAPCR was raised from "actual care, control, and possession" to "exclusive care, control, and possession" for at least six months. Step-parents and long-term caregivers who shared parenting responsibilities but did not exercise exclusive care lost standing under this amendment. Applies to suits filed on or after September 1, 2025.

HB 2340 amends Tex. Fam. Code § 107.105 (effective September 1, 2025): custody evaluators must complete at least three hours of training specific to children with intellectual or developmental disabilities during the two-year period preceding any evaluation involving such a child. Courts may not admit an evaluation report when the evaluator lacked this training. Exclusion is upon timely objection; no retroactive application to evaluations completed before September 1, 2025.

HB 3181 amends Tex. Fam. Code §§ 156.107, 157.165, 157.167, 157.168: a conservator found in contempt for denying court-ordered possession who has three or more prior contempt findings for the same conduct faces four mandatory civil consequences — the pattern constitutes a material and substantial change of circumstances (automatic modification ground); no community supervision; no attorney's fee waiver; compensatory possession must be twice the duration of the denied periods. This is a civil enforcement mechanism only.

SB 2794 amends Tex. Penal Code § 25.03 (effective September 1, 2025): interference with child custody is now a criminal offense applicable to any parent — not just noncustodial parents. First and second offenses are Class C misdemeanors (up to $500 fine). Third offense or enticement of the child out of state is a state jail felony (180 days–2 years; up to $10,000 fine). This closes the prior loophole that only penalized noncustodial parents.

How do I file for divorce in Travis County Texas?

File an Original Petition for Divorce with the Travis County District Clerk, pay the filing fee of approximately $300–$350, and serve your spouse unless they sign a Waiver of Service. The 60-day waiting period begins on the filing date regardless of how quickly the case resolves.

How long does a divorce take in Travis County?

An agreed uncontested divorce takes 60–90 days minimum due to the statutory waiting period. A contested divorce involving property disputes or child custody typically takes 6–18 months in Travis County. Cases that go to trial take longer.

How is property divided in a Texas divorce?

Community property is divided in a manner the court finds "just and right" under Tex. Fam. Code § 7.001 — which does not always mean 50/50. Fault in the breakup of the marriage, disparity in earning capacity, and the needs of any children are factors courts consider in Travis County property division.

How does child custody work in a Texas divorce?

Texas uses conservatorship rather than custody. Joint Managing Conservatorship is presumed under Tex. Fam. Code § 153.131. The primary dispute is usually which parent has the exclusive right to designate the child's primary residence and what possession schedule applies — standard or expanded standard under Tex. Fam. Code § 153.317.

What changed about protective orders in Texas divorce cases in 2025?

Under SB 1559 (effective September 1, 2025), a family violence protective order now prevails over any conflicting temporary or final order in a pending divorce. A party or the court may file a motion to transfer the protective order proceeding into the divorce court — once that motion is made, transfer is mandatory unless safety concerns prevent it. Previously, transfer was discretionary.

What changed about child support in Texas in 2025?

C.S.H.B. 2643 (effective September 1, 2025) raised the net monthly resources cap from $9,200 to $11,700. If the obligor earns above $9,200 net per month and the prior order was calculated at the old $9,200 ceiling, the cap increase alone may support a modification petition filed after September 1, 2025.

What does a Travis County divorce cost?

An agreed divorce with no children runs $1,500–$3,500 in attorney fees plus filing costs. A contested divorce settled at mediation runs $8,000–$20,000. A contested divorce through trial runs $25,000–$75,000 or more depending on complexity.
