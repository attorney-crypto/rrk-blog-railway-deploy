Civil Contempt and Judgment Defense in Texas

Texas civil contempt and judgment defense is the legal work around what happens when a creditor or opposing party asks the court to enforce its prior order against someone alleged to be non-compliant. Contempt power in Texas is broad on paper, but it is also constrained by due process, by the requirement that the order be specific enough to be obeyed, and by the distinction between civil and criminal contempt.

The window to prepare a defense is short, the consequences of a contempt finding (which can include jail time in some circumstances) are serious, and the procedural rights of the alleged contemnor are real and substantive.

Civil vs. criminal contempt — why the distinction matters

Texas distinguishes civil contempt (coercive — designed to make the contemnor comply with the order, such as paying owed child support or turning over property) from criminal contempt (punitive — designed to punish past disobedience). The procedural protections differ, and the available outcomes differ.

In civil contempt, the contemnor traditionally "holds the keys to the jailhouse" — performance of the required act ends the confinement or sanction. In criminal contempt, the sanction is fixed (a determinate jail term or fine) regardless of later compliance, and the alleged contemnor has additional procedural rights akin to those of a criminal defendant.

Many contempt proceedings include both civil and criminal components — for example, jail time for past missed child support payments (criminal) combined with continued coercive sanctions for any continuing non-payment (civil). The mixed proceedings require careful handling.

Procedural rights of an alleged contemnor

Notice. Texas Rule of Civil Procedure and constitutional due process require specific notice of the conduct alleged to be in contempt, the order alleged to be violated, and the time and place of the hearing. Vague notice is itself a defense.

Opportunity to be heard. The alleged contemnor has the right to appear, to present evidence, and to cross-examine witnesses. In a criminal contempt proceeding (or the criminal portion of a mixed proceeding), the right to counsel includes the right to court-appointed counsel where confinement is a possible outcome.

Burden of proof. The movant has the burden of proof. The standard depends on the nature of the contempt — clear evidence of violation, with some criminal contempt issues requiring proof beyond a reasonable doubt.

Right against self-incrimination. In any proceeding that could result in confinement, the alleged contemnor has Fifth Amendment protection against being compelled to testify.

Common defenses in Texas contempt proceedings

Order is not specific enough to be obeyed. Texas case law requires that an order be "clear, definite, and unambiguous" enough that a reasonable person understands what the order requires. Orders that say "act in good faith" or "reasonably cooperate" without specifying conduct are often unenforceable by contempt.

Inability to comply. In civil contempt for non-payment, inability to pay is a defense if it is shown by the alleged contemnor's evidence. In family-law support contempt, Texas Family Code §157.008 provides a specific affirmative defense based on inability to pay despite reasonable efforts.

Compliance with the order. The simplest defense is that the contemnor actually did what the order required. This requires documentation and, sometimes, a paper trail through the opposing party or court records.

Procedural defects. Insufficient notice, insufficient specificity in the show-cause papers, failure to follow required hearing procedure, or denial of counsel where confinement is on the table can each support a defense.

Sanctions and remedies in Texas contempt

Coercive sanctions (civil contempt) typically include fines and confinement until performance. Texas Government Code §21.002 sets out general contempt limits — a court generally cannot impose punishment more severe than $500 or six months' confinement per act of contempt without a jury (with some exceptions).

Punitive sanctions (criminal contempt) are determinate. They are fixed at the time of the order regardless of later compliance. Constitutional protections — including the right to a jury where the sentence exceeds six months — apply.

Some contempt orders also include fee-shifting (the contemnor pays the movant's attorney's fees) and additional remedial requirements (turn over specific property, sign specific documents, perform specific acts). Each is subject to the same specificity and due-process requirements.

Can I go to jail for civil contempt in Texas?

Yes, in some circumstances. Civil contempt for failure to comply with a court order — including failure to pay court-ordered child support or to comply with a turnover order — can result in confinement until compliance. The Texas Government Code §21.002 generally caps confinement at six months per act of contempt without a jury, with exceptions. Where confinement is a possible outcome, the alleged contemnor has the right to counsel.

What if I genuinely cannot afford to pay?

Inability to pay is a defense in civil contempt for non-payment. In family-law support contempt, Texas Family Code §157.008 provides a specific affirmative defense of inability to pay despite reasonable efforts to obtain employment and resources. The defense must be proven by the alleged contemnor's evidence; it does not happen automatically.

Can a court hold me in contempt for an order I cannot understand?

Texas case law requires that an order be clear, definite, and unambiguous enough that a reasonable person understands what is required. Vague orders that direct the parties to "act in good faith," "cooperate," or "act reasonably" without specifying conduct are usually not enforceable by contempt.

What rights do I have at a contempt hearing where jail is on the table?

Notice of the specific conduct alleged, the right to be present and to present evidence, the right to cross-examine witnesses, the right to counsel (court-appointed counsel where the alleged contemnor is indigent and confinement is a possible outcome), and the right against self-incrimination. The burden is on the movant to prove the contempt.
