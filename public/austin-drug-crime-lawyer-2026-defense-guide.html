What to do first after a Texas drug arrest

First, use your right to remain silent. Clearly state that you want to speak with a lawyer and that you will not answer questions, and then stop talking to officers, investigators, or anyone else about the facts of the case.

Second, preserve any evidence that may help later. Save text messages, call logs, rideshare records, medical or prescription records, and contact information for anyone who was present during the stop, search, or arrest; do not delete or alter anything, even if you think it looks bad.

Third, contact a qualified Austin drug crime lawyer as soon as you can so that counsel can review the arrest report, bond conditions, and any search or seizure issues, and then begin communicating with the prosecutor before important decisions are made about charges and plea offers.

Why drug crime cases are different from other criminal cases

Drug cases often turn on search and seizure law more than on factual disputes about what happened. The legality of a traffic stop, a pat-down, a search warrant, or a consent search can determine whether the drugs are admissible in court, and suppression of that evidence can sometimes end the case.

They are also highly dependent on how the substance is classified and weighed. Texas Health and Safety Code Chapter 481 organizes controlled substances into penalty groups, and the penalty range can change dramatically based on small differences in weight or on whether the allegation is possession, delivery, or manufacture.

Unlike some other offenses, drug charges frequently involve questions about constructive possession, where the state claims you had control over drugs that were not physically on you (such as in a car or house). In those cases, the link between you and the drugs, including your statements and where you were in relation to the substance, is often heavily litigated.

Finally, drug cases can be different because collateral consequences are wide-ranging. A plea that avoids jail might still trigger immigration problems, school discipline or scholarship consequences, employment or licensing concerns, housing issues, or future sentence enhancements, so the long-term impact has to be considered, not just the immediate punishment.

Who may be charged under Texas law

Texas law allows prosecutors to bring different drug charges depending on your alleged connection to the substance and your role in any transaction. Simple possession cases usually allege that a person knowingly or intentionally possessed a controlled substance listed in Texas Health and Safety Code Chapter 481, such as Section 481.115 for Penalty Group 1 substances.

Someone accused of selling, delivering, or manufacturing a controlled substance can face more serious charges. Manufacture or delivery of a Penalty Group 1 substance, for example, is covered in Texas Health and Safety Code Section 481.112, which has higher penalty ranges that increase with weight and certain aggravating factors.

Other people can be charged based on their involvement in a broader drug operation. A person may face charges for possession with intent to deliver, conspiracy, or even engaging in organized criminal activity under Texas Penal Code Section 71.02 if the state claims they worked with others to commit drug offenses.

Landlords, drivers, or roommates are sometimes charged when drugs are found in a shared space or vehicle. In those situations, the key issue is usually whether the state can prove that the person knew about the drugs and had care, custody, or control over them, rather than just being physically nearby.

Evidence that can decide a Texas drug case

In a drug case, the first category of critical evidence is how officers came into contact with you and the substance. Patrol car video, body-worn camera footage, dispatch logs, and 911 recordings can show whether the stop, detention, or search matched what is written in the police report and whether officers had reasonable suspicion or probable cause.

The second category is the physical evidence itself: the substance, its packaging, where it was found, and how it was tested. Lab reports, chain-of-custody records, photographs of the scene, and any surveillance video can all affect whether the state can prove the substance is what they claim, in the quantity they allege, and tied to you personally.

Third, your own words and the words of others can be decisive. Statements made to police, text messages, social media posts, and witness testimony may help or hurt, which is why it is important not to talk about the facts of the case with anyone but your lawyer. In some cases, a preservation letter to law enforcement, the district attorney, or third parties such as businesses with surveillance footage can help ensure that potentially useful records are not overwritten or destroyed before they can be reviewed.

Finally, medical and prescription records can be important when controlled substances are lawfully prescribed, such as opioid pain medication or ADHD medication. Those records can show that your possession was authorized and may help negotiate or defend against certain charges if they match the drug and quantity at issue.

Texas punishment ranges for drug offenses

Texas sets punishment ranges for drug crimes largely based on the type of substance, the penalty group, and the amount involved. For example, possession of a Penalty Group 1 substance under Texas Health and Safety Code Section 481.115 can range from a state jail felony for less than one gram up to a first-degree felony for larger quantities.

General punishment ranges for Texas crimes are found in the Penal Code. A state jail felony is punishable by 180 days to 2 years in a state jail facility and a fine up to $10,000, while a first-degree felony can carry 5 to 99 years or life in prison and a fine up to $10,000, under Texas Penal Code Sections 12.35 and 12.32. For certain large-quantity drug cases, the Health and Safety Code sets its own enhanced ranges that can exceed those Penal Code limits. For example, Texas Health and Safety Code Section 481.115(f) provides for life or 10 to 99 years and a fine of up to $100,000 for possession of 400 grams or more of a Penalty Group 1 substance. Courts can also impose probation, community supervision, fines, drug treatment, and other conditions.

Certain factors can increase potential punishment. Prior convictions can enhance the range under Texas Penal Code Chapter 12, and drug-free zone enhancements can apply if the offense occurred near schools or other protected locations under Texas Health and Safety Code Section 481.134. The presence of a firearm, allegations of distribution to minors, or related offenses such as evading arrest can also affect how a case is charged and negotiated.

Texas deadlines can waive defenses

Even though drug charges are criminal, timing still matters. Under the Texas Code of Criminal Procedure, many drug offenses are subject to a three-year statute of limitations for felonies not otherwise listed, under Article 12.01, and a two-year limitation period for misdemeanors under Article 12.02. These rules apply to when the state must file charges, not how long a case can remain pending.

More practically, several important defense issues are subject to deadlines inside the case. If the court sets a pretrial hearing or filing deadline under Texas Code of Criminal Procedure Article 28.01, motions to suppress and other preliminary matters may need to be filed by the court's deadline, often at least seven days before the pretrial hearing unless the court permits later filing. Missing a court-ordered deadline can limit or waive certain arguments.

Evidence can also disappear quickly. Surveillance video, store recordings, rideshare data, and phone records are often kept for a limited time. Promptly sending preservation letters and having counsel request and subpoena records early in the case can make a significant difference in what evidence is available for your defense.

When to call an Austin drug crime lawyer

You should consider contacting an Austin drug crime lawyer immediately after any arrest or citation involving controlled substances, including possession of small amounts of illegal drugs, prescription medications without a clear record, or THC concentrates. Early counsel can address bond conditions, no-contact orders, and testing requirements that may affect your daily life.

What happens at my first court date in a Texas drug case?

The first court setting is usually for you to formally receive the charges, review the complaint or indictment, confirm your bond conditions, and start the process of discovery. You typically do not resolve the case that day, and your lawyer will use the early settings to obtain police reports, video, and lab records before advising you on options.

Is marijuana still illegal in Austin under Texas law?

Under current Texas law, possession of usable quantities of marijuana is still a criminal offense under Texas Health and Safety Code Section 481.121, although some local jurisdictions may have policies about low-level enforcement. Hemp with low THC levels is treated differently, but officers and labs may still test substances when they cannot tell the difference on sight.

Can a prescription protect me from a Texas drug possession charge?

A valid prescription can be a legal basis for possessing many controlled substances, but it must match the drug, dosage, and person who is in possession. Texas Health and Safety Code provisions in Chapter 481 recognize lawful prescriptions, so providing accurate pharmacy and medical records to your lawyer is important if your case involves medications.

Will a Texas drug conviction have consequences beyond the criminal sentence?

Yes. Texas drug convictions can carry collateral consequences in addition to any sentence imposed by the court. These can include immigration effects for noncitizens, professional license issues, school or scholarship consequences, public housing eligibility issues, and possible driver's license consequences depending on the offense and current Texas law. Because these consequences vary widely and change with statutory amendments, anyone facing a drug charge should discuss the long-term impact with counsel before entering any plea.

What is a drug-free zone enhancement in Texas?

A drug-free zone enhancement increases the penalty range for certain drug offenses committed in, on, or within a specified distance of places like schools, playgrounds, or youth centers. Texas Health and Safety Code Section 481.134 sets out these enhancements, which can add mandatory minimums or elevate the degree of the offense.

Can Texas drug charges be dismissed if the search was illegal?

If a court finds that police violated your constitutional rights during a stop or search, it can suppress the evidence obtained, including the drugs. Without that evidence, prosecutors may not be able to proceed, and dismissal can become likely, but this usually requires a timely motion to suppress and a hearing under the Texas Code of Criminal Procedure.

How long does a Texas drug case usually take?

Drug cases can take anywhere from a few months to more than a year to resolve, depending on the seriousness of the charge, lab testing delays, the court's schedule, and whether the case goes to trial. Your lawyer will typically use this time to investigate, negotiate, and file any necessary motions before you decide whether to accept a plea or proceed to trial.

Can I get a Texas drug charge off my record later?

In some situations, a person may qualify for an expunction or an order of nondisclosure under the Texas Code of Criminal Procedure and Government Code if the case is dismissed, results in not guilty, or meets specific deferred adjudication criteria. Whether you will qualify later depends heavily on how the current case is resolved, so it is important to consider record consequences at the time of plea discussions.
