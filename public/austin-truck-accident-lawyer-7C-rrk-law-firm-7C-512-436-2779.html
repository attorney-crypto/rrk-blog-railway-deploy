Federal Trucking Regulations Can Lead to Liability Issues

Federal Motor Carriers Safety Administration and the Department of Transportation have regulations that a truck driver needs to abide by. The regulations include specific rules that truckers must follow:

Federal Trucking Regulations - Insurance

Federal regulations set the minimum liability insurance commercial motor carriers must carry:

$750,000 for for-hire carriers of property with a gross vehicle weight rating of 10,001 or more pounds

$5 million for for-hire and private carriers of hazardous substances with a gross vehicle weight rating of 10,001 pounds or more

$1 million for for-hire and private carriers of oil with a gross vehicle weight rating of 10,0001 pounds or more

Employee Handbooks for Truck Drivers

If you're filing an injury claim against a truck driver or truck driving company, it's useful to consult the employee handbook for truck drivers. These handbooks contain details about company policies and procedures that all truck drivers must adhere to while on the job.

The handbooks offer clear instructions that cover safety protocols, loading and unloading techniques, regulations on truck driver logs, and handling different types of cargo. They also provide tips on avoiding dangerous behaviors like distraction and fatigue while driving. Additionally, the handbooks provide helpful contact information in case of emergencies or accidents.

Most driver employee handbooks begin with a section explaining the company's mission statement and values. The handbook outlines workplace conduct policies and expectations that all employees must follow, including dress codes, communication rules, drug and alcohol testing requirements, and others. The handbook also covers topics specific to trucking operations, such as load securement methods and safety measures for loading docks.

Handbooks often have sections that focus on safety regulations such as hours-of-service (HOS) laws and hazardous material transportation rules. These rules are strictly enforced to prevent any harm to the public resulting from careless driving practices.

Driver employee handbooks often contain sections that explain how to file grievances or complaints against management or fellow drivers. This is done to promote a safe work environment that is free from discrimination or harassment.

These documents are essential in ensuring that the company adheres to a standard of care in transporting loads. This also helps hold truck drivers accountable for following certain regulations that are necessary for safe transportation operations across the country.

What Happens If I'm Partially Responsible for a Truck Accident Case?

In Texas, there is an equal fault system where fault in an accident is shared among all parties involved. Under a Texas statute, individuals can seek damages even if they are partially at fault for an accident.

However, if a person is found to be partially liable for an accident, their damages may be reduced according to their percentage of fault. Under Texas law, if an individual's liability is greater than 51% of the total fault, they will not be able to recover any damages. This is known as the modified comparative negligence rule.

What happens in Texas if someone sues you in a car or truck accident?

The damages awarded in a car/truck accident lawsuit are divided into two categories: economic and non-economic. Economic damages are intended to compensate for losses related directly to money, such as medical bills or lost wages due to the inability to work after an accident. Non-economic damages are intended to compensate for the pain and suffering caused by the accident, including emotional distress, disfigurement, or loss of quality of life due to disability. In some cases, punitive damages may also be awarded by a court if the defendant’s conduct was particularly egregious. Punitive damages are meant to further punish the defendant and deter them from engaging in similar behavior in the future.

No matter which type of damages are awarded, the court will take into account other factors such as negligence or fault before making a final decision.
