Your Divorce Journey: Key Steps and Considerations

The journey to divorce often begins with a single step—filing a petition with the district clerk in Travis County. Once this step is taken, the divorce process starts to unfold, culminating in a court-issued divorce decree that lays out the terms and conditions of the separation. Throughout this journey, a seasoned divorce lawyer can provide the much-needed guidance to ensure a fair division of assets and represent your interests during negotiations and trial appearances. From property distribution to child custody and support, an experienced divorce lawyer evaluates all key issues to ensure your rights are protected.

In Texas, there are certain residency requirements to fulfill before filing for divorce. To file for divorce in this county, at least one spouse must have been a resident for six months and lived in the specific county for 90 days. After filing, there’s a mandated waiting period of at least 60 days before the divorce can be finalized.

Understanding the Types of Divorce

With your attorney selected and your financial plan in place, the next step is understanding the various types of divorce. Divorce in Texas may proceed as:

- Uncontested divorce

- Contested divorce

- Mediated divorce

- Collaborative divorce

- High net worth divorce

Each type has its unique processes and considerations.

Different types of divorces include:

  • Uncontested divorce: when the parties agree on all terms of the separation.
  • Contested divorce: when the parties are unable to come to an agreement, potentially leading to a trial.
  • Mediated divorce: involves a neutral third-party mediator to help spouses reach a fair resolution.
  • Collaborative divorce: both parties work together to expedite the process, leading to a more efficient resolution.
  • High net worth divorce: can involve additional complexities such as the division of significant assets or business interests.

    • Understanding these types of divorces can help you and your attorney develop a strategy that best suits your situation.

    Uncontested Divorce

    An uncontested divorce, where both parties agree on all terms of the separation, can be an agreed divorce where both parties consent, or a default divorce where one party does not respond. The principal characteristic of an uncontested divorce is the lack of disagreement between spouses about the terms of their divorce. This type of divorce typically offers benefits such as reduced legal costs and a faster resolution compared to other types.

    While this process may seem straightforward, it’s still vital to have legal representation to ensure your rights are protected and the agreement is fair and equitable.

    Contested Divorce

    In contrast to an uncontested divorce, a contested divorce arises when the parties cannot agree on one or more key issues, leading the process to potentially proceed to a trial setting. When a mutual agreement fails to materialize, the case is tried before a judge or jury for final judgments, which can lead to increased costs and longer timeframes. However, many spouses opt for negotiation over court trials to preserve co-parenting relationships, maintain privacy, and reduce the costs associated with a contested divorce.

    In no-fault divorce cases, unlike fault-based divorce cases, one party does not need to allege that the other has engaged in behavior justifying the divorce, such as adultery, which if proven, can significantly impact the divorce proceedings.

    Mediated and Collaborative Divorce

    Mediated and collaborative divorces prioritize cooperation and negotiation over litigation. In a mediated divorce, spouses or their lawyers collaborate with a neutral third-party mediator. The aim is to reach fair resolutions to disputes through the process..

    On the other hand, a collaborative divorce occurs when both parties agree not to take the matter to court, and instead, work together to reach a resolution. In these types of divorces, the emphasis is on harmonious resolution and cooperation, which can lead to a more amicable post-divorce relationship, particularly beneficial when children are involved.

    High Net Worth and Complex Divorces

    High net worth and complex divorces involve additional layers of complexity due to the significant assets or business interests at stake. These cases involve meticulous division of all community assets, which can be highly challenging when dealing with high-value assets such as businesses, real estate, and investment portfolios. If a business is involved, a detailed analysis is required to correctly categorize and divide the business interests between spouses. Other complexities arise from the need to establish what constitutes community property versus separate property, especially when retirement accounts are involved.

    Retirement savings accumulated during the marriage are also subject to division by the court, adding another layer of complexity to high-net-worth divorces.

    If children are involved in a divorce, navigating child custody and support issues becomes a paramount concern. In joint managing conservatorship cases, the custodial parent is entitled to receive child support payments from the non-custodial parent, and the court can modify child support orders to reflect changes in circumstances.

    A divorce lawyer specializing in family law at a divorce law firm can provide guidance on family law matters, leveraging their experience to protect a client’s rights and ensure the legal process honors the child’s best interests.

    Child Custody

    Child custody is a critical issue in any divorce involving children. In Texas, family courts prioritize the child’s best interest when determining custody, without considering a parent’s marital status, gender, religion, or race. Custody decisions take into account the child’s physical and emotional needs, as well as prospects for intellectual and social development.

    Judges evaluate the following factors when determining custody arrangements:

    - Parenting abilities, including involvement in the child’s education and capacity to cooperate with the other parent

    - Stability and security of each parent’s home environment, such as income stability and household living arrangements

    - The child’s own wishes, if they are of sufficient age and maturity to express them

    These factors are key considerations in custody arrangements.

    Child Support

    Child support is another crucial aspect of divorce involving children. In Texas, child support is calculated based on the non-custodial parent’s financial situation. All child support payments are processed through the State Disbursement Unit located in San Antonio. When setting up child support payments, the recipient must complete the ‘Record of Support Order 1828A’ form with the assistance of the District Clerk. This ensures that the child support payments are appropriately managed and disbursed.

    While the process may seem complex, having an experienced divorce attorney can ensure the process runs smoothly and in your child’s best interest.

    Property Division and Spousal Support in Divorce

    Another significant aspect of divorce is property division and spousal support. In Texas, the law recognizes community property, which refers to property acquired during a marriage. Separate property refers to assets individually owned by a spouse before the marriage or received as a gift or inheritance, which typically remain with that spouse after divorce. The division of assets in a divorce is based on a principle that aims for a roughly equal split, rather than a strict 50/50 division, taking into account each spouse’s circumstances and separate property.

    Spousal support in Texas, or alimony, is determined by factors such as the spouse’s income, length of marriage, behavior during the marriage, and the grounds for divorce.

    Community Property vs. Separate Property

    Understanding the difference between community and separate property is crucial in a divorce. Community property in Texas includes all property and income earned by either spouse during the marriage, while separate property comprises assets owned before the marriage or acquired as gifts, inheritance, or personal injury settlements. Community property may include income from employment, real estate, vehicles, and contributions to retirement accounts made during the marriage.

    Separate property, on the other hand, may include:

    - real estate or vehicles owned before the marriage

    - gifts are specifically given to one spouse

    - inheritance

    - personal injury settlements pertaining exclusively to one spouse

    - In a divorce, Texas law requires courts to make a ‘just and right division’ of both community property and debts incurred during the marriage, holding both spouses potentially responsible.

    Equitable Distribution

    Texas courts uphold the property and debt division agreement made by spouses if it is considered ‘just and right’. This means that the division is not necessarily a strict 50/50 split, but rather aims for a roughly equal distribution taking into account each spouse’s circumstances. This can include factors such as each spouse’s earning potential, the duration of the marriage, and who is taking primary custody of the children.

    Furthermore, a spouse can seek reimbursement when community funds have been used to make payments, such as a mortgage, on the other spouse’s separate property. This principle of equitable distribution ensures that the division of assets and debts is fair and just in the context of each spouse’s situation.

    Spousal Support (Alimony)

    Spousal support, also known as alimony, can be granted in Texas divorces and is a critical aspect of the divorce process. This monetary support may be ordered as part of dividing community property and is determined by factors such as:

    - the spouse’s income

    - length of marriage

    - behavior during the marriage

    - the grounds for divorce

    The goal of spousal support is to mitigate any unfair economic impacts of a divorce by providing ongoing income to a lower-wage-earning or non-wage-earning spouse. Discussing and negotiating spousal support with your attorney is a critical area where their expertise can advocate for fair agreements.

    Preparing for Court Hearings and Negotiations

    Once you’ve navigated through the initial phases of the divorce process, it’s time to prepare for court hearings and negotiations. The initiation of a divorce hearing in Austin, TX, involves filing an Original Petition for Divorce and paying the necessary filing fees. Following the filing of the divorce petition, there’s a mandatory waiting period of at least 60 days before a final hearing can take place.

    During this period, the following steps are required:

  • Mandatory case review at the Travis County Law Library
  • Filing a completed Vital Statistic Form alongside the Final Order in a divorce case
  • Scheduling a court hearing with the Court Administrator and providing notification to the opposing party if the divorce petition is contested and has been answered
  • Gathering concrete evidence, such as financial records and witness statements, to support your position during these hearings.

    • Gathering Evidence

    Gathering evidence is a crucial part of preparing for court hearings and negotiations in a divorce process. Some types of evidence that are pivotal in Texas divorces include:

    - Documentary evidence like bank statements and property deeds

    These types of evidence are considered in custody decisions.

    An effective legal strategy in divorce cases involves combining documentary and testimonial evidence to influence court decisions. Presenting a compelling case that establishes the best interest of the child is essential to prevail in a custody dispute.
