Grounds for Filing Divorce in Texas

Texas has a law that allows for no-fault divorces. If a couple agrees mutually to end their marriage, they can still get divorced even if they don't agree on everything. While it may be helpful to prove fault in some cases, fault is not always required.

Moreover, in Texas, the family code outlines seven different reasons for divorce, including both fault and no-fault options. It's important to again highlight that fault does not need to be proven in order to obtain a divorce in Texas. However, the grounds you choose may impact the outcome of your divorce settlement.

INSUPPORTABILITY

LIVING APART

CONFINEMENT TO A MENTAL HOSPITAL.

THE ROLE OF FAULT-GROUNDS IN TEXAS DIVORCE

The distinction between fault grounds and no-fault grounds is relatively simple. Under fault grounds, one spouse is held responsible for the divorce.

Nonetheless, in Texas, it is not necessary to establish fault grounds to get a divorce. Still, if you declare certain fault grounds such as adultery, and the judge finds your argument unconvincing, the divorce may be granted on other no-fault grounds, like insupportability.

On the other hand, if cruelty or any other fault grounds appear during the case, the judge may still grant the divorce even if you initially filed on the no-fault grounds of insupportability.

The four different fault grounds in Texas:

CRUELTY

ADULTERY

FELONY CONVICTION.

ABANDONMENT.

Rules Concerning Children During Divorce Proceedings - Standing Orders

When you file for divorce in Travis County, you will receive a Standing Order that outlines specific rules regarding your children. According to these rules, neither parent can take the children out of Texas without the written consent of the other parent.

Additionally, children cannot be taken out of school without both parents' permission in writing. If a court finds that a parent has violated these rules, they may take action against them. For example, hiding your children or moving without permission would be considered a violation.

What about a high net-worth divorce?

Divorces involving wealthy individuals can be complicated, as all assets earned during the course of the marriage are divided fairly, regardless of how they were earned.

Request of Temporary Restraining Order

In case of challenging situations, you may require temporary limitations. This document specifies certain rules for a restricted duration regarding your interactions with your spouse, which mandate both parties to maintain a respectful demeanor regarding property, spending of community assets, and custody, among other scenarios fact specific to your particular matter. Notably, the TRO presents a unique order that pertains to temporary custody issues, and safeguards any shared assets outside the normal protection of the typical Travis and Williamson County standing orders.
