Navigating Custody Battles in the State

of Texas

Custody battles in the state of Texas can be challenging and emotionally draining experiences. The stakes are high, as parents want to ensure that their case's outcome is in their children's best interest. It's essential to understand the legal considerations of a custody battle, the types of custody, how to go about establishing custody, and the process for modifying an existing agreement. This guide will cover those topics in detail so that you can be better informed and prepared when navigating a custody battle in Texas.

Types of Custody

When it comes to custody battles, there are two main types of custody that can be awarded: legal and physical.

  • Legal custody: This type of custody determines which parent will have the authority to make decisions related to the child's well-being, including healthcare, education, and extracurricular activities.
  • Physical custody: This type of custody dictates where the child will live and which parent they will spend the majority of their time with.
  • Joint/Split custody: This type of custody is when the child lives with each parent for an equal amount of time, typically determined by the court.

    • Establishing Custody

    The process of establishing custody in Texas starts with filing for custody. The parent or guardian who files for custody is called the petitioner, while the other party is referred to as the respondent. During this process, several factors are taken into consideration, such as:

  • Each parent's mental and physical health: The court looks at each parent's ability to provide a stable and secure home for the child.
  • The child's emotions and preferences, if they are old enough to express their opinion: The court may consider the child's preference if they are 12 years or older.
  • The parent's history of domestic violence or abuse: This is an important factor, as the court will want to ensure that the child's safety is of utmost importance.
  • The stability of each parent's home environment: This includes factors such as financial stability, living arrangements, and job stability.

    • Once a court has heard both parties, they will determine the type of custody that is in the best interest of the child and issue an appropriate order.

    Modifying a Custody Agreement

    Once a custody agreement is in place, it may be modified if either party wishes to do so. Reasons for modifying a custody agreement may include changes in living arrangements, employment status, or a significant change in the child's best interest.

    For a modified agreement to be legally binding, it must be approved by the court. The process for making changes to an existing agreement involves both parties filing a petition, the court hearing evidence, and ultimately approving or denying the modifications.

    Final Thoughts

    Navigating a custody battle in Texas can be a complicated and emotionally-draining experience. It is important to understand the types of custody, the process for establishing and modifying custody agreements, and the legal considerations that go into court-ordered decisions. It is also essential to be aware of the resources available for guidance and support throughout the process.
