2025–2026 TEXAS LAW

Texas law permits modification of a final custody order under Tex. Fam. Code § 156.101 only when modification serves the best interest of the child and circumstances have materially and substantially changed since the original order was rendered, the child is at least 12 and has expressed a preference to the court, or the primary conservator has voluntarily relinquished primary care for at least six months. Tex. Fam. Code § 156.102 imposes a one-year bar on modification suits following entry of the original order except in relocation cases and emergencies. Travis County modification cases are filed in the same district court that issued the original order. 2025 Legislative Updates — 89th Texas Legislature (Effective September 1, 2025):

C.S.H.B. 2643 increased the child support cap on net monthly resources from $9,200 to $11,700. If a prior order was calculated at the $9,200 ceiling and the obligor earns above that amount, the cap increase may independently support a modification petition filed on or after September 1, 2025.

HB 2350 amends Tex. Fam. Code § 102.003: non-parent caregivers seeking to file a SAPCR must now demonstrate exclusive care, control, and possession of the child for at least six months — up from the prior "actual" care standard. Step-parents and other caregivers who shared parenting with a parent may no longer meet the threshold. Cases filed before September 1, 2025 under the prior standard may face exposure if modification is sought on or after that date.

HB 3181 amends Tex. Fam. Code §§ 156.107, 157.165, 157.167, 157.168 — civil "three strikes" enforcement: when a conservator has three or more prior contempt findings for denying court-ordered possession and is found in contempt again, four consequences are mandatory: (a) the pattern constitutes a material and substantial change supporting modification without additional proof; (b) no community supervision; (c) no waiver of attorney's fees; (d) compensatory possession must be twice the duration of the denied periods. Applies symmetrically to both primary and non-primary conservators.

SB 2794 amends Tex. Penal Code § 25.03 — criminal three-strikes enforcement: interference with court-ordered child custody is now a criminal offense applicable to any parent (previously noncustodial only). First and second violations are Class C misdemeanors ($500 fine). Third offense or enticement out of state is a state jail felony (180 days–2 years). Criminal convictions under SB 2794 may be leveraged in family court to support conservatorship modification.

SB 1559 amends Tex. Fam. Code §§ 81.012, 85.026(b), 85.064: a valid family violence protective order prevails over any conflicting divorce or SAPCR order. Transfer of the protective order proceeding to the SAPCR court is mandatory upon motion (previously discretionary), unless transfer would negatively impact the safety of a protected person. Consolidation is not automatic — a motion is required.

SB 1120 extends protective orders to run until two years after the final custody order.

HB 2340 amends Tex. Fam. Code § 107.105 (effective September 1, 2025): in modification cases involving children with intellectual or developmental disabilities, the custody evaluator must have completed at least three hours of disability-specific training in the two years preceding the evaluation. Courts may not admit a non-compliant evaluation report upon timely objection.

What qualifies as a material and substantial change in Texas?

Relocation of the primary conservator significantly impairing the other parent's access, documented family violence or abuse occurring after the original order, significant deterioration in the child's school performance or mental health traceable to the current arrangement, drug or alcohol abuse by the primary conservator developed post-order, and consistent denial of court-ordered access. A job change or general parenting disagreement does not meet the threshold.

What is the new "three strikes" law for custody interference in Texas?

Two separate 2025 laws address this. HB 3181 (civil): a fourth contempt finding after three prior findings for denying possession triggers mandatory modification grounds, no community supervision, no fee waiver, and doubled compensatory possession time. SB 2794 (criminal): any parent who interferes with the other's court-ordered possession faces Class C misdemeanor charges on the first and second offense, escalating to a state jail felony on the third offense or if the child is taken out of state. Both laws are effective September 1, 2025 and apply symmetrically to custodial and noncustodial parents.

Can a 12-year-old choose which parent to live with in Texas?

A child 12 or older may sign a document expressing a preference which the court must consider — but the judge is not bound by it. The preference is one factor among many. The best interest of the child standard controls regardless of the child's stated preference.

How do I file for custody modification in Travis County?

File a Petition to Modify the Parent-Child Relationship in the Travis County District Court that issued the original order. Filing fee is approximately $300–$350. If there is an emergency basis — family violence, imminent relocation, sudden change in living conditions — a temporary orders hearing can be requested immediately.

What changed about child support in Texas in 2025?

C.S.H.B. 2643 (effective September 1, 2025) raised the net monthly resources cap from $9,200 to $11,700. For an obligor earning above the old $9,200 cap, the increase alone may constitute grounds for a modification petition. Courts retain discretion to order above-cap support.

Can I modify custody if my ex is moving out of Austin?

Yes. Relocation more than 100 miles from the other parent triggers specific notice requirements and gives the non-primary conservator the right to request a modification hearing under Tex. Fam. Code § 156.102. Relocation is one of the most common and most litigated modification triggers in Travis County.
