How is intoxication Defined?

In the state of Texas, under criminal law, "intoxication" is defined as a condition in which an individual lacks normal mental and physical functioning due to the introduction of alcohol, drugs, or any combination thereof into their body. Furthermore, intoxication may also be evidenced by having an alcohol concentration level of 0.08% or higher in one's bloodstream.

How bonds work in jail?

To make sure an accused person can be released from custody while awaiting trial, a bail amount is paid out. This sum of money might come directly from the defendant in the form of a personal bond or alternatively, through hiring a bondsman with whom to arrange for a bail bond. In other words, the overall purpose remains: ensuring that those charged receive recourse and freedom until their hearing date arrives.

For example, if you are arrested and the court sets your bail at $60,000, then you can either pay it in cash or use a bail bond. A bail bond is an agreement between the accused individual and a bonds company that pledges that they will ensure the defendant appears for their trials. In this instance, rather than paying out of pocket for the full amount of money owed to be released from custody, you can enlist assistance from a credible bondsman who has promised to cover it on behalf of yourself or someone else.

If you post the bail bond, the court will release you under pending trial, and if not posted, you will remain in custody until after the trial. Texas bail bond companies must have a license from the state of Texas for the bail bond to be accepted.

Is my driver's license automatically suspended after I am charged with a DWI in Texas?

The short answer is no, not right away. And there are certain circumstances, like when there is a blood draw, where your license may not be suspended until the results are back from the lab. This is a complicated situation and you should consult a DWI lawyer immediately if you have a DWI charge.

Generally speaking though, you will have 15 days to make an ALR hearing request to challenge any license suspension. Your temporary license will be good for 40 days or as long as it takes for you to get an ALR hearing.

Texas Driver License Suspension - generally

The driver’s license suspension that you receive after a DWI conviction is a criminal penalty that is separate from the administrative license suspension that many people receive after being arrested on suspicion of DWI. In Texas:

- A first-time DWI conviction includes a driver’s license suspension of 90 days to one year.

- A second or third DWI conviction includes a driver’s license suspension of 180 days to two years.

- A conviction for DWI with a child passenger in your vehicle can result in a suspension of as long as 180 days.

Following a DWI conviction, the time that your driver’s license was already suspended under the administrative penalty can be credited toward your criminal suspension.

What is the meaning of deferred adjudication in Texas and how is it related to an order of nondisclosure?

Deferred adjudication as it relates to the criminal court system, is a specific form of court-mandated supervision, otherwise known as “probation.” It allows the accused to accept responsibility for their crime without officially having it go on record. But unlike in a jury trial, deferred adjudication must be granted by a judge; thus requiring both the prosecutor and defendant to agree upon waiving one. So who is eligible?

If you are hoping to receive Texas DWI deferred adjudication, there are a few considerations that must be taken into account. Unfortunately, if you have any prior DWIs on your record or held a blood alcohol level of .15 or more at the time of arrest, this option is not available for you. Additionally, those with commercial licenses and anyone responsible for injury/death as well as property damage beyond their own vehicle will also fail to qualify. Thus it is important to remember these exceptions when deciding whether deferred adjudication will work in your favor.

Don't be misled - pleading guilty is still necessary for deferred adjudication in Texas. You and your DWI attorney must appear before the judge, even though no jury trial applies here, to accept guilt. This procedure differs from the DWI pretrial diversion program that you may participate in without having to attend court at all.

You must go through a probationary period, during which you will be tested for drugs and may have to fulfill community service requirements or install an ignition interlock device. If you successfully pass the trial phase, then your punishment can be reduced or even eliminated in spite of having admitted guilt.

Does a deferred adjudication show up on a background check in Texas?

A common misbelief concerning deferred adjudication in Texas is that completing it will automatically erase the charges from your record. This isn't true. Unlike pretrial diversion, concluding a deferred adjudication won't make you eligible for expunction - only nondisclosure. As a result, merely part of your criminal history (not all) can be removed after finishing this program.

Seeking a non-disclosure order is essential if you wish to protect yourself from the damaging repercussions of your DWI charge. Although it will not be concealed from government agencies or law enforcement, an NDA can shield you against prejudices when applying for mortgages, rental properties, homes, and jobs. However, obtaining such an order isn't automatic - your lawyer must file a petition with the court once your deferred adjudication has been finalized in Texas.

Does deferred adjudication count as a conviction in Texas?

Through successful completion of a deferred adjudication program, the charges against you will be "deferred" and ultimately dismissed. However, your record may still show evidence of them, and obtaining a nondisclosure order is essential to keep that information private. On the other hand, should you fail to fulfill probation requirements it will count as an official conviction on your record. Additionally, if another DWI offense arises in the future it would appear as such on any background checks - resulting in harsher penalties than those associated with first-time offenders.

DWI Accidents and Aggravated Penalties

Approximately 40% of all fatal car accidents in Texas involve drivers who are intoxicated. In order to deter drivers from getting behind the wheel while drunk, the consequences of DWI can be aggravated when an accident occurs. State district attorneys have the discretion to pursue a wide range of criminal penalties after you've caused a DWI accident. The charges you could potentially face will depend on (1) your level of intoxication and (2) the extent of the damage caused by the crash.

DWI Accidents Resulting in Property Damage

Not all DWI crashes will result in a personal injury or death of another person. However, accidents resulting in property damage are still considered to be a serious threat to public safety. If you are involved in a DWI accident that results in property damage, you can be charged with DWI in addition to other criminal offenses. Crimes that are often charged in addition to DWI include reckless damage and/or criminal mischief.

Reckless Damage

Reckless damage, as defined in Texas Penal Code Sec.28.04, occurs when you “recklessly damage or destroy property” belonging to another person without their consent. Conduct is considered reckless when you know, or should have known, the potential consequences of your actions but “consciously disregard substantial and unjustifiable risk(s).”

Destruction is a Class C Misdemeanor, punishable by a fine of $500. If you are responsible for the destruction of another person's property, you may also be responsible for paying restitution to the property owner.

Criminal Mischief

Criminal mischief, as defined in Texas Penal Code Sec.28.03, occurs when you “intentionally or knowingly damage or destroy the tangible property” of another person. When is conduct considered “intentional” or “knowing” for the purposes of Texas state law?

Intentional: Conduct is defined as intentional when you have a “conscious objective or desire to engage in the conduct or cause the result.”

Knowing: Conduct is defined as knowing when you “are aware of the nature of your conduct or the circumstances that exist.” In other words, you are “reasonably certain” that your actions will cause a certain result.

So, you can face criminal charges for criminal mischief if there is evidence to show that you (a) intended to damage property or (b) were reasonably certain that driving while drunk would cause property damage.

Criminal mischief can be charged as a misdemeanor or a felony in Texas. The seriousness of the charge will depend on the extent of the damage that is caused in your DWI accident:

Class C Misdemeanor:

- Property damage less than $100

Class B Misdemeanor:

- Property damage between $100 and $750

Class A Misdemeanor:

- Property damage between $750 and $2,500, or

- Causing impairment or interruption of a public water supply

State Jail Felony:

- Property damage between $2,500 and $30,000;

- Property damage to a fence that was used to contain livestock or game animals; or

- Property damage causing impairment or interruption of public communication, public transportation, public gas supply, or public power supply.

Third-Degree Felony:

- Property damage between $30,000 and $150,000

Second-Degree Felony:

- Property damage between $150,000 and $300,000

First-Degree Felony:

- Property damage > $300,000.

The more damage your accident causes, the more severe the criminal penalties will be.

DWI Accident Causing Bodily Injury

Drunk driving crashes that result in bodily injury can result in additional criminal charges for intoxication assault. Intoxication assault, as defined in Texas Penal Code Sec.49.07, occurs when you operate a vehicle while intoxicated and cause serious bodily injury to another person by accident or mistake.

Serious bodily injury is defined to mean:

- Injury that creates a substantial risk of death,

- Injury that causes permanent disfigurement, or

- Injury that causes protracted loss or impairment of a bodily member or organ.

Intoxication assault is a Third-Degree Felony, punishable by between two and ten years in prison and $10,000 in criminal fines.

DWI Accident Resulting in Death

If your Austin DWI accident results in another person's death, you may face additional criminal charges for intoxication manslaughter. Intoxication manslaughter, as defined in Texas Penal Code Sec.49.08, occurs when you drive while intoxicated and “by reason of that intoxication cause the death of another by accident or mistake.”

The victim of your deadly DWI crash can be anyone, including your own passengers, other drivers, bicyclists, motorcyclists, or even pedestrians. Victims do not necessarily have to die on impact in the DWI crash. You can also face criminal charges for intoxication manslaughter if a victim suffers fatal injuries and dies at a later time and date. The bottom line is that you can face criminal charges as long as your drunk driving conduct caused someone's death.

Intoxication assault is a Second-Degree Felony in Texas, punishable by between two and twenty years in prison and $10,000 in fines.

Defending DWI Charges in Austin

Have you been involved in an Austin accident and accused of driving while intoxicated? It's important to understand that an accusation does not necessarily mean that you will be charged with and/or convicted of a crime. The state must be able to prove that you are guilty of a crime beyond a reasonable doubt. This is an extremely high burden of proof that can be difficult to satisfy if you assert a strong defense.

Defenses that may be helpful in defending criminal charges for DWI property damage, assault, and manslaughter include:

- You were not under the influence of drugs or alcohol,

- Results of the breath and/or blood test are inaccurate and/or invalid,

- No property was damaged in the crash,

- No one died or suffered an injury in the crash,

- The state has gathered evidence against you in violation of your Constitutional rights, and/or

-You have been mistakenly identified or falsely accused of a crime.
